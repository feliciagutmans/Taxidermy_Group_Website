The Natural History Museum

A look into our Mammals Hall for #TaxonomyTuesday: closest to the camera is our Asian elephant, Elephas maximus, and in the background you can see our African elephants, Loxodonta africana. These two species represent the only extant genera, not only in their taxonomic family, but also in their entire order. Elephas and Loxodonta represent rather different branches of the Elephantidae family tree: Asian elephants are more closely related to extinct mammoths (Mammuthus) than their African cousins. The wider order of Proboscidea contains a multitude of other extinct elephant-like animals, including the well-known mastodons (Mammutidae) and the rather more unusual Gomphotheres (Gomphotheriidae) - both also on display in the Mammals Hall. Gone, but still groovy

Although it may seem that Taxidermy, or preserving dead beings, is an antiquated practice, it seems to be all the range for young contemporary artists today. Whether creating large frankenstein-esque sculptures to a multi-headed chicken, to animals floating in formaldahide, the taxidermised is back. After all, who says that just because it's dead, it can't still be "fresh"?
