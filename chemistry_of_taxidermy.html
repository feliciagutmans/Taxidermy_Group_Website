Formaldehyde: The Chemistry of Preserving Specimens

'The Physical Impossibility of Death in the Mind of Someone Living'- Damien Hirst, 1991

What is formaldehyde and why do we need it to preserve things?

Formaldehyde (systematic name methanal) is the simplest aldehyde and is an organic compound. Formaldehyde was the first compound to be detected in the interstellar medium, meaning that formaldehyde can be detected in outer space. Formaldehyde is a very common building block in the synthesis of other compounds and molecules in organic chemistry. Small specimens, up to 100g, can be fixed whole in formaldehyde by submerging them in 10% formaldehyde solution. The animals should be submerged in the position in which they are to remain permanently because they will harden once submerged. The ratio of formaldehyde to the animal's body should be 12:1 to ensure good fixation in the formaldehyde.

Are there any other ways we can preserve specimens other than using formaldehyde?

There are several ways in which we can preserve specimens, other than using formaldehyde. These include: 1) Preservation of alcohol: the specimen can be preserved in alcohol (70-90%), although it is recommended that the intestines are removed before storage. 2) Preservation by cooling or freezing: removing the skin with the insulating fur before cooling or freezing can help to cool down the animal's body quicker.
